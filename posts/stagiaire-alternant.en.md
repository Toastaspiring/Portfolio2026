Ok so I'm a Master's-1 apprentice. Translation: I do my Master's INSIDE the company, 3 weeks on-site 1 week at school, and at the end of the year I hand in a report that maybe two humans will read — me and my mentor, and honestly I'd put even odds on the mentor.

Last week the company informs me, all smiles: "good news, you've been assigned an intern." An intern. **Bachelor year 3.** On MY project. That I'll be mentoring.

Bachelor 3. One year behind me. Next year, he'll be exactly where I am now — hopefully with a slightly better grip on the Central Limit Theorem than I had.

![Spider-Man pointing at Spider-Man](https://i.imgflip.com/1tkjq9.jpg)

Kickoff meeting. I walk through the project with a confidence I 0% possess. He takes notes. I look at him. He looks at me. And then it hits me: his questions are word-for-word the ones I was asking 12 months ago. His "ok but like, what actually IS the CLT" — I remember the EXACT moment I asked my stats prof that, with the same slightly embarrassed face, silently praying the answer would be short. Spoiler: it was not.

What this situation has really given me is a deep appreciation for how understanding builds up in layers, and every layer feels uniquely humiliating until the next one shows up:

![Galaxy brain: levels of CLT understanding](https://api.memegen.link/images/gb/learning_the_clt/using_the_clt/teaching_the_clt/getting_asked_the_exact_question_i_asked_last_year.jpg)

And in 1-on-1s I have to perform Professional Experience I don't have. "So in the pro world..." — bro, one year ago I was *literally you*, staring at my first numpy array like it owed me money, trying to understand why `shape` and `size` weren't the same thing.

Ok, jokes aside (for two seconds). It's surprisingly formative. Having to explain something forces you to actually understand it. And realizing you genuinely UNDERSTAND things that read like runic glyphs 12 months ago is strangely therapeutic. Imposter syndrome loses a little ground every time the intern comes back with "oh yeah it converges, thanks!".

The lesson: when you don't know, say you don't know. The lethal trap when you're "the reference" with a one-year head start is trying to fake it. Spoiler: it shows. And you forget that 12 months ago you were the exact same person flailing at the exact same proof.

So to all future apprentices about to inherit an intern one year behind them: breathe, it's actually kind of fun. You're mentoring your past self — that's a rare privilege. Most people would pay good money to time-travel and yell at themselves about convergence proofs.
